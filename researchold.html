Ocean Resources and Renewable Energy at UMass Amherst
Variable Geometry Oscillating Surge Wave Energy Converter

June 2020 - Present

Role: Graduate Student Researcher at UMass Amherst

Project Partners: University of Massachusetts Amherst; National Renewable Energy Laboratory

Description: We are developing a wave energy converter which employs variable geometry modules to control the governing hydrodynamics of the body. As the lead graduate student, I employ a combination of boundary element modeling in ANSYS AQWA, time domain simulations in WEC-Sim, and analytical hydrodynamic models to assist in the sizing and optimization of the device geometry. Presently, I am designing the experimental fixture, designing the scale model, and selecting wave probes, optical tracking equipment, and sensors. In the coming months, we will fabricate the scale model, implement control and power takeoff systems, and conduct experiments in our wave tank.

  1. N. Nguyen, J. Davis, C. Burge, N. Tom, K. Thiagarajan. "Optimizing power generation of a bottom-raised oscillating surge wave energy converter using a theoretical model." European Wave and Tidal Energy Conference. September 2021.

    The preliminary sizing of an oscillating surge wave energy converter (OSWEC) is an iterative process that relies on knowledge of the relevant hydrodynamic coefficients for a given geometry. Often, the complex definition of the device geometry requires coefficients to be obtained through experiments or numerical solvers such as WAMIT (boundary element method). These techniques demand significant user and computational effort, therefore inhibiting the fine scale parametrization of objective dimensions. In this study, a theoretical model, originally presented in Michele et al. (2012), is developed and demonstrated to efficiently optimize the power production for an OSWEC device (subjected to certain environmental conditions) with variations in device widths, heights, and distances from the seabed. Assuming negligible device thickness, the OSWEC motions are modelled as a bottom-raised two-dimensional paddle in regular waves using potential flow theory formulated in elliptical coordinates. The solutions to this diffraction-radiation problem are obtained using Mathieu functions with appropriate boundary conditions. The resulting potentialsare then used to derive frequency-dependent expressions for the added mass and radiation damping coefficients, along with wave excitation magnitude in surge, pitch, and coupled surge-pitch motions. Expression of these terms in closed forms enables the hydrodynamics of the device to be characterized over a range of geometric properties in a quick and computationally efficient manner. The theoretical model is employed to maximize the time-averaged output power while maintaining or reducing the hinge reactionforce, with variations in device dimensions, wave frequency and amplitude. Good agreement is shown with results from WAMIT and experiments for hydrodynamic coefficients and response amplitude operators for various configurations.
